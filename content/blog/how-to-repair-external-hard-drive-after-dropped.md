## Step 1: Understand the Problem with the Drive

Before we try to fix the issue with the drive, we want to understand what the exact problem is. Is the hard drive clicking, or beeping, or does it not have power whatsoever? Depending on this, we can take the next steps toward repairing the drive in order to get our data back.

- **Clicking:** If the hard drive is making clicking sounds after it was dropped, the issue is either bad read/write heads OR media damage. If the issue is the heads, you can attempt a head swap and regain access to the data. If the issue is media damage (damage to the platters where the data is stored), then the data would most probably be unrecoverable.

- **Beeping:** If your hard drive is making beeping sounds after it was dropped, the issue is called "Stiction." This is where the read/write heads have crashed on top of the platters, not allowing the platters to spin, and thus making a beeping-like sound. Please do not rush to watch YouTube videos on how to repair this problem — there is more to the process than just the physical repair.

- **Drive Spins Normally, But Does Not Mount:** It's important to put your ear to the drive and listen carefully. If you hear the drive spinning up and it's not making any unusual sounds, but it's not mounting on the computer, this is a pretty good sign. Chances are that the issue is a damaged file system or firmware related. If it's firmware related, you may need to seek a professional as you will need a special tool to repair the problem (PC-3000 by Ace Laboratory).

## Step 2: Inspecting the Inside of the Drive

This step is very important. Please do not rush and skip steps. Data recovery is not about speed — it's about precision. You only get one good chance at recovering the data. One small mistake can make your data unrecoverable forever.

**IMPORTANT:** In this step, you are going to open the actual drive cover (not the enclosure for the drive, but the actual metal drive cover where the platters are stored). **WE DO NOT RECOMMEND YOU DO THIS YOURSELF**, but if you are determined to get it done, please follow all steps carefully. Make sure you have all the tools you need — if you don't have the exact screwdriver (usually T5, T6, T8), please order the correct tools and don't risk stripping any screws.

### If Your Drive is Clicking

If your issue is clicking, first take a look at the platters to make sure there are no noticeable scratches or contamination on the top surface. We always use a green light (which can be purchased from Amazon) to make sure the surface is clean. If the surface is clean, check the read/write heads to make sure they are not physically damaged or contaminated. You will need a head comb tool to safely remove the heads.

- **Step 1:** Place the head comb on the read/write heads. Slowly and gently press the tool onto the heads. Once the tool is secure, proceed to Step 2.
- **Step 2:** Remove the two screws that are securing the hard drive cable connector to the drive case. Carefully pull the connector up out of its place.
- **Step 3:** Remove the screw holding the magnet in place. Using tweezers, get a good grip on the magnet from the rear and firmly raise it up off of the head assembly.
- **Step 4:** Depending on what model drive you have, there might be a parking brake that will prevent the heads from coming off of the ramps. Carefully remove this pin using tweezers.
- **Step 5:** Remove the entire headstack assembly from the drive. Keep your eye on the sliders (tip of the heads) during removal. Make sure you are holding the heads all the way back and away from the parking ramps.
- **Step 6:** Inspect the sliders (tiny black readers) under a microscope. These sliders should be super clean and shiny — not a speck of contamination. If they are clean, your chances of recovering the data are much higher.

### If Your Drive is Beeping

If your issue is beeping, the first thing to do is observe the situation inside the drive in detail. Don't touch anything yet. Look for scratches or contamination on the top surface using a green light. If there are no scratches or contamination, you are ready for the next step.

- **Step 1:** In the center of the drive, find the screw(s) holding the platters down. Get the correct screwdriver ready. What we're trying to accomplish is to bring the heads that are stuck on the platters back onto the parking ramps in one smooth motion — done by spinning the platters with the screwdriver while simultaneously using tweezers to pull the heads back.
- **Step 2:** Practice this motion before doing it on the actual drive. With your right hand, turn the screw with the screwdriver and with your left hand use tweezers to bring the heads straight back and off of the platters.
- **Step 3:** Once you have successfully removed the heads from the platters, **DO NOT** just put the drive cover back on and turn on the drive. If the read/write heads are physically damaged or if there is contamination on the sliders, powering on the drive will destroy the data forever. Remove the heads and inspect them under a microscope.
- **Step 4:** Follow all of the steps from the "Clicking" section above to remove and inspect the heads.

### If Your Drive Spins But Doesn't Mount

If after dropping your drive it spins normally but doesn't mount on your computer, you have the best chances of recovering the data.

- **Step 1:** Connect your drive to the computer. Although the drive does not mount normally, check if the drive is being recognized by the BIOS. On Mac, go to "Disk Utility." On PC, go to "Disk Management." Do you see your drive listed?
- **Step 2:** If you see your drive listed, pay attention to how it's seen by the computer. Does the computer recognize the correct capacity? If the size is recognized correctly, that's an excellent sign — continue to Step 3. If your drive is not listed at all, power down the drive and seek a professional. The issue may be firmware related, and firmware repairs are inexpensive when handled early — but opening the drive for a "mechanical" fix that turns out to be firmware can triple the cost.
- **Step 3:** Applies ONLY if the BIOS recognized the drive's correct capacity. You can try running data recovery software on the drive. We **do not recommend this** as the software could spread media damage, but if you are determined, proceed cautiously.
- **Step 4:** Download data recovery software and start the scan. Monitor the entire scanning process — do NOT leave it unattended. If the scan gets stuck at certain points, stop immediately and safely power down the drive.
- **Step 5:** If the scan completes successfully without errors, begin saving the recovered data. Keep monitoring the process throughout.

## Step 3: Attempting a Head Swap

By this point, you have inspected the inside of the drive, removed the heads, inspected the sliders, and are now ready to replace them with a compatible donor. A compatible donor is another drive that is a near-identical part. On the drive's label, match:

- Drive Model Number (exact)
- Part Number (exact)
- Country Manufactured (exact)
- Date of Manufacturer (within 3 months)
- DCM (for WD drives)

The more you match the drive's label, the higher the chances the donor part will work. The process of the head swap is identical to how you removed the heads from your original bad drive. Simply follow all steps in reverse to reassemble the drive. Key points when reassembling:

- Use tweezers to hold the headstack assembly as you're putting it back in the drive.
- Carefully hold the heads all the way back (away from the parking ramps) as you're tightening the bottom screw.
- Once tightened, carefully allow the heads to go back into their position on the ramp — there should be no force required.
- Gently hold the heads in their place on the parking ramp, then put the parking brake pin back and carefully replace the magnet.
- Tighten the drive cover screws in a zig-zag order and in small increments.
